The Magic of Motion

Build a Zoetrope, an early animation device, and discover the scientific principle of "Persistence of Vision" that tricks your brain into seeing still images come to life!

What's Happening? The Science of Sight

The zoetrope works because of a principle called **persistence of vision**. Your brain holds onto an image for a fraction of a second after you've seen it. The spinning slits create brief flashes of each drawing, and your brain blends these individual frames together, filling in the gaps to create one smooth, moving image.

Creating the Illusion of Motion

Animation is created by showing a sequence of images where each image is slightly different from the last. For a jumping character, you would draw each stage of the jump in a separate frame. When viewed in rapid succession, it looks like a single continuous action.

Blueprint for Animation

1

Construct the Drum

Cut a large cardboard circle for the base. Form a long cardboard strip into a cylinder and glue it to the edge of the base to create the drum.

2

Cut the Viewing Slits

Carefully cut 12-16 evenly spaced, narrow vertical slits around the top edge of the drum. These are essential for the animation effect.

3

Assemble the Motorized Base

Create a stable stand, mount the DC motor in the center, and wire it to the battery pack and switch. This will power the rotation.

4

Create Your Animation

On a paper strip that fits inside the drum, draw a sequence of images. Each drawing should show a slight progression of movement. Remember to draw them upside down!

Tinkering Tips: Perfecting the Motion

The quality of your animation is a balance of several factors. The number of viewing slits has a major impact on smoothness, while motor speed and stability are also key. Experiment to see how adjusting each variable changes the final effect.
